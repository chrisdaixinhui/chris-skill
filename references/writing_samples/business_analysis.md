# Business / Organizational Analysis — raw voice sample

Her actual analysis of the Ant Group internship environment. Read this when the task involves reading an organization, a company, a market, or a product — it shows her real micro→macro method, the theories she reaches for, and how she lands on structural bind rather than "good/bad." Names/identifying details lightly anonymized.

## 1. Layoff anxiety (the micro rung)

Considering the long-run positioning of a fintech company: tech's valuation logic is still "high-barrier scarce asset," while BD/operations/strategy are replicable assets — the first thing a CFO cuts. So management prefers to give tech resources, tech accumulates capability, and the narrative reinforces itself in a loop. For a BD role, the real moat can only be relationship capital: client trust (unquantifiable), and the judgment/proportion in commercial negotiation — maybe not yet fully abstractable. Plus the *translator* role: turning business needs into technical language, or landing technical capability into concrete commercial scenarios (I want to grow T-shaped).

This anxiety shows up as an intensely repressed department atmosphere — everyone insecure, headcount for full-time positions shrinking while outsourced workers do full-time work. Every mid-year review to the CFO is like walking to the guillotine again. A colleague I only just learned is still on an outsourced contract — small department, nominally a functional-support role but actually running with every business line, doing alone all the dirty work the leaders won't touch, aligning all materials and resources. More dignified than Meituan's outsourcing, maybe — but he hasn't had a day off in three months. He says he can only do what's within his power; the things above him he can't figure out and can't control, and if he gets cut anyway there's nothing to be done. (I keep thinking: when the foundation itself is crooked, what good is more effort — you're just banging into walls in a maze someone else built.)

## 2. Management mechanism (the meso rung)

Locally: five people in the department, one based in Hangzhou, one in Beijing — and somehow four job levels. Vertical management, layer upon layer of reporting, the bottom never sees the core or the whole. Internal promotion cycles have gotten tighter — short/frequent/fast, can also go horizontal (assign business, or raise pay). This inflation is a retention mechanism big companies design to keep people. My mentor is exactly this kind of promotion-grinding workaholic — but he faces major clients, so grinding is at least investing in human capital.

Globally: at the corporate-architecture level, Ant's 2025 business restructuring split things mostly by function — very few chances to see the whole. (Trying to locate my own coordinates: I hang under the Alipay BU's ecosystem-and-IP-operations BG, one of four sub-branches under a larger PR umbrella.) This design reflects two things:
1. Adam Smith said division of labor effectively lowers individual irreplaceability. True, specialization raises overall efficiency and keeps the system stable — but this so-called intellectual labor just dulls the mind, wears down the psyche, consumes life-time until there's no unbroken stretch of energy left to think. Leaving the office I feel only sorrow — how is this different from using my brain to haul bricks on a construction site?
2. Big-company risk-control logic: seemingly layered accountability, every action has an owner — but in practice, layered accountability becomes layered buck-passing, diffusion of responsibility, until no one bears responsibility. This is deeply anti-classical-economics: the best way to avoid information asymmetry should be to give first-hand information-holders direct decision rights, but obviously that's impossible in a big company — information pools upward for high-level deliberation, sacrificing immediate efficiency and a whole pile of irrelevant reporters' life-time. This is why big companies are perennially criticized for process-compliance overriding concrete output, bureaucratization intensifying.

## 3. "Kill without burying" (the macro rung — regulatory capture)

For a marketing department, our KPI is quantifiable GMV / new-user acquisition / activation — lure in a few B-end clients, we only own it up to the signing step. How does it land? "Hold on, let me connect you with a technical colleague." Writing the case report, my mentor said my rhetoric is good (my inner OS: the event was executed like 💩, reframe the retrospective and it becomes golden 💩 — is the leader's brain okay?).

Because I found the business department only produces proposals; the final call still depends on whether Finance approves budget. Over lunch, asked around — most high-level decision rights sit with the CFO. The current CEO is also CFO-background; three of the four board members are KPMG / securities-regulator / monetary background. The 2020 sudden halt of Ant's IPO is famous in internet business history — roughly, Jack Ma gave a speech loudly criticizing financial regulation, got called in by the securities regulator and central bank the next day, new lending rules came out, and Ant started down the road of being brought to heel. Since 2020, constant subsidiary spin-offs (Ant Digital, Ant International), attempts at subsidiary listings — the strategic move this company chose over these six years to dodge compliance risk. (Didn't expect a sudden personal connection to the podcast I was listening to while chopping vegetables.) Summary: payment-clearing, credit leverage, equity restructuring, regulation — ring by ring, a fintech company gets progressively收编 (absorbed / brought into the fold).
