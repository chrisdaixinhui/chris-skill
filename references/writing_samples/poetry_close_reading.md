**Close Reading Analysis**

"Cold in the earth, in a deep snow", the past was buried in the extreme
cold, time was like a severing wave that tended to wash away all the
memory of the only love she ever knew. In 'Remembrance', Emily Brontë
expresses the strong grief of the beloved ones in youth, and her stern
courage in the painful necessity of living. In stanzas 5 to 6, she
explored the agonizing transition from a past bliss of life to a
traumatizing existence without joy. By creating a desperate feeling
through using repetitive rhythmic iambic pentameter and famine
metaphors, she illustrated the stasis of grief that would never recover
and the existence form of disciplined survival without the "fed of joy".

The fifth stanza of the 'Remembrance' expresses the nature of absolute
grief and the permanence of loss through various rhyming techniques,
successfully creating the freezingly traumatizing atmosphere for the
poem. Firstly, Brontë employs anaphora, which is the repetitive use of a
negative word at the beginning of successive lines, such as "No later
light" and "No second morn." Two complete negations create the sense of
total finality and emotional stagnation. The speaker's world was
suddenly dark and incapable of creating new hope from that point. In
contrast, the absence of a morn and light suggests that all the hope and
chance of renewal had gone with her only love, transitioning to the next
two lines in the stanza. Especially when comparing to the anaphora of
the next two lines beginning with "All my life's bliss", "No" and "All"
are both absolute words, creating a tension based on the contrast of the
past and the present. This is the desperate emphasis on Brontë's joys
and bliss, which had all been buried, would never come back again.

Furthermore, considering the structural technique of the whole poem, the
iambic pentameter is vital to the feeling of despair. This is a
10-syllable rhythm consisting of five \"iambs\", famously used by
Shakespeare, creating a natural, heartbeat cadence that adds emotional
emphasis to the poem. The feminine ending of the words "heaven" and
"given", Brontë uses the unstressed syllable of "ven" that causes the
line to end with a falling sigh, which fades in the air. The
traumatizing sighing in the rhyme mirrors the speaker's attitude that
all her happiness had been graved with her beloved ones. While her body
was physically existing on the earth, her spiritual hope had already
died, with her heart broken.

The transition from stanzas 5 to 6 marks a significant shift in
emotions, signaled by the powerful turn of the word "But". While the
previous stanza depicted a world of total desperation with no hope,
stanza 6 rebuilds the grief of memory as a disciplinary sustenance.
Brontë used a bunch of metaphors from opposing emotional perspectives to
emphasize endurance in life. Starting from the statement that existence
is now "fed without the aid of joy", the underlying assumption is that
"joy" is the soul's natural food. Just as the presence of "the days of
golden dreams" in the first line, "golden dreams" symbolizes the past
time when her lover was alive. However, the soul still had the
biological necessity to continue, with all the happiness being deprived.
The spiritual famine is related to the deprivation of joy. These, on the
contrary, point to the strengthened existence being fed by the
bitterness of memory and stern discipline.

The spiritual starvation is a consequence of the "all the severing wave
of time" as mentioned in the previous stanzas. Just as the fifteen wild
Decembers buried the grave of her youth love cold in the earth, the
"world's tide" of time bore her, forced her to move forward, away from
the mourning of her love. Her soul was sternly independent of the
barren, empty landscape over the brown hills. She must "wean her young
soul". To "wean" is to accustom oneself to managing without something of
which they have excessively depended. This word choice highlights the
slow, agonizing process of ending the life-sustaining connection. By
personification of "Despair" in the 6th stanza, she further demonstrates
the resilience towards life of the struggling soul, specifically, in a
permanent state of cold and mechanical survival, finding a stern power
that even "despair" was powerless to destroy.

Brontë's "Remembrance" is a poem about the stern discipline of survival
without joy. It's a testament to living with the traumatizing
desperation and grief. Through the structural use of iambic pentameter
and the starvation metaphors, Brontë creates a rhythm that pulses
beneath the declarations of absolute grief and the weaning soul.
Ultimately, the speaker learnt to cherish a joyless existence, to "not
indulge in memory's rapturous pain", and her soul found a divine power
to confront the world's tide. While the specific meter is a unique charm
of the English language, the core emotional truth of her famine symbols
remains a universal bridge across different languages, demonstrating the
resilience of a struggling soul.
