I was doing The Importance of Being Ernest by Oscar Wilde, Act2, the
topic of satire and farce. My close reading discussion mainly centered
around two parts. First, there is the diary A component, both Cecily's
and Gwendolen's, that constantly arises as a key prop throughout the
whole scene. The characters' attitudes toward their dairy is one thing
worth examining, reflecting Wilde's sharp wit on the absurdities and
artificiality of Victorian society. Secondly, I analyze the specific
lines around Cecily's comments on Algernon's hypocrisy. Her words, along
with Alge's reaction, reveal a vivid display of the so-called
"Bunburyism,", which already occurred in previous scenes. These elements
come together to touch the hidden theme of inverted morality, which
satirizes the upper classes and their social conventions, deconstructing
the seriousness of morality, marriage, and honesty.

In the second act, the diary is an important element that constantly
appear for more than 14 times. For both ladies' diaries, the ways they
treat the diaries showed slight contrasts, while the core of their
attitudes pointed towards Victorian society's "fake" nature,
coincidentally. Starting from Cecily's diary, from the very beginning of
the scene, she said that "I keep a diary to enter the wonderful secrets
of my life." Cecily explains the diary's usage to herself. "To enter the
wonderful secrets" already creates a slight contradiction, since
"wonderful" means things that are pleasing and exciting, while "secrets"
may represent the authenticity of private hidden facts. In a normal
condition, Diaries are always used to keep the chronological truth of
lives, which are factual, private, and serious. But Cecily elaborates on
her point by saying, "It usually chronicles the things that have never
happened, and couldn't possibly have happened. I believe that Memory is
responsible for nearly all the three-volume novels that Mudie sends us."
Her attitudes toward diary are somewhat subversive, "the things that
have never happened, "couldn't possibly have happened", both emphasize
the things she's written in her diary are all unrealistic, romance
fantasy from her own mind, which serve as a prelude for the future
plots. Also, "Mudie's three-volume novels" made a joke of the famous
Victorian circulating libraries, supplying long novels that are
sentimental and exaggerated. It is easy to tell that Cecily takes memory
as a source of fiction, which is actually fake and untrustworthy. As
stated by one of the classmates, this also creates the tension with Miss
Prism's words, "Memory is the diary that we all carry about with us."
Memory won't be truthful all the time.

Discussing whether the development of the plots that reveal more of her
diary's nature and what the author wants to express, we also relate back
to the other romance components we discussed previously. Just as my
classmate Callie's mentioning of Cecily's imagination of all the love
letters and the fake engagement, my classmate Ruby pointed to the scene
where Cecily even bought herself the engagement ring using the name of
Mr Ernest, a man she never met before. "I determined to end the matter
one way or the other, and after a long struggle with myself, I accepted
you under this dear old tree here. The next day, I bought this little
ring in your name." The speech uses serious and realistic language, like
"determined", "under long struggle", "accepted you", "the next day", to
create a satirical effect of the character. These fully imagined
emotional experiences are the so-called truth of Cecily's wonderful
secrets of her life. Wilde satirized Victorian romance as a narrative
structurally romance solo performance.

For Gwendolen's diary, she appeared to treat it as something
entertaining, satirizing the reality of life in the upper-class society.
She said, "I never travel without my diary. One should always have
something sensational to read in the train." The word "sensational" is
worth pondering. Its initial meaning relate with arousing quick,
intense, and superficial interest. Gwendolin here consumed her diary as
"something to read in the train", a kind of reading material like a
newspaper or novels in a public setting. This is also against the
initial meaning of a diary about one's own life. Wilde uses these words
to satirize the private life in Victoria's era, which is performed and
fake. Character's exaggerated behaviors naturally reveals of a code of
behavior that Victorian convention pretends to disguise and ignore. Just
as stated by several of the classmates, we could relate the performing
nature of their lives back to the first scene, at Jack and Lady
Bracknell's conversation around his engagement with Gwendolen. Lady
Bracknell's questions to Jack centered only around the family
background, social classes, and economic status. Never mentioning
whether they love each other or not, the satirical moment seems to be
Lady Bracknell's reaction towards the truth that Jack was found in a
handbag. This can be something undoubtedly unacceptable in Victorian
marriages.

The second aspect of my discussion centers around Cecily's and Algenon's
conversation about "hypocrisy. As Algenon met Cecily using the name of
Ernest, he said, "Oh! I am not really wicked at all, cousin Cecily. You
mustn't think that I am wicked.", emphasizing " not really wicked at
all", revealing Algernon's desire of leaving good impression on Cecily.
However, Cecily answered meaningfully as she took Alge as Uncle Jack's
brother, who was doing all the wicked things in the town, "If you are
not, then you have certainly been deceiving us all in a very inexcusable
manner. I hope you have not been leading a double life, pretending to be
wicked and being good all the time. That would be hypocrisy." Her words
centered around "hypocrisy", which is the practice of claiming to have
moral standards to which one\'s own behavior does not conform.
"deceiving", "in an inexcusable manner", "pretending to be wicked and
being really good" shows her stance that she expect Ernest to be wicked,
Ernest's being not wicked would be a dishonesty. This reveals the
inverse logic of morality, since here, "pretending to be back while
being good" is the actual hypocrisy. Alge reacted to her words by
"\[Looks at her in amazement\]. Oh! of course, I have been rather
reckless." Here, "\[Looks at her in amazement\]. Oh!" His amazement
revealed his startling for telling this lie, which he quickly adapted to
the expectation of Cecily by stating would rather be reckless. During
the discussion of why Alge has such a large reaction, one classmate
suggested his situation of moral inconsistency because he is currently
pretending to be someone else. Alge's situation was paradoxical to his
previous behavior in Act 1. He used to use Bunbury, a fake friend, as an
excuse to escape all the parties he didn't want to attend while avoiding
hypocrisy. It epitomizes the central irony of the play, for the
bunburying Algernon becomes a hypocrite himself by pretending to be
somebody he is not.

Through the intertwined motifs of the diary and hypocrisy in Act II,
Wilde successfully constructs the layered satire using clever wordplay
and settings. Cecily's and Gwendolen's diaries, though treated
differently, are both detached from the traditional function of private
writing as a record of truth. In Cecily's accusation of hypocrisy, the
conventional ethical standards are completely overturned. Ultimately,
Wilde uses the conventions of the comedy of manners to deconstruct the
performing nature of marriage, honesty, and respectability in the
Victorian century.
