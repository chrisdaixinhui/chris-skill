**What Writing Does for Lives Not Supposed to Be Written About**

Chris Dai

\"Remember, when you get your business degree, pay the labor more.\"

Sam said this to me the day I told him I was quitting. He is a chef at
the school pizza station --- in his forties, a pizza chef for more than
twenty years, starting at Papa John's. He is, without question, an elite
in the knowledge realm of making pizza. He knows nearly everything: the
exact timing of the large oven, the precise humidity of the flour, and
the exact sequence of placing pizzas in the oven to ensure even heat
distribution. He can throw the dough into the sky and catch it
bare-handed with ease. Like dancing, he told me, his performance
fluctuates with his emotional condition on any given day.

He taught me how to make pizza because I asked him eagerly. The tomato
sauce, he explained, should circle in the spoon at a specific angle,
directly tangent to the dough. This is the best control system for
distributing the sauce evenly. For a good pepperoni pizza, the ten
ounces of cheese in the recipe should be placed with your hands held
high above the dough, more cheese toward the edges, because the dough
will rise when heated, and the cheese will slide down the slope toward
the center. Sam knows everything about pizza. And just as he knows
everything about pizza, he tries to know every young person who passes
through his station. He knew where I was from. He was curious about my
academic life. And we were curious about his.

He is from North Carolina. He got divorced and lost custody of his son.
Though he loves his son deeply, his ex-wife remarried quickly, and Sam
was only allowed half a day each weekend with him, which he called the
best day of every week. He likes to play video games with his son. Sam
was the only person at the dining hall who worked four days a week
instead of five. He joked, with a kind of self-deprecating humor, that
he was the only one permitted to do so. Later, we found out why: he had
a chronic digestive disease that put him in the hospital for an entire
month.

I did not know this while he was gone. I just knew he was absent, and
that I had to make twenty-two pizzas alone one night to keep the station
running. Only when Sam was not there did I understand how easily things
fall apart without him. The oven caught fire because the charcoal ash
had not been cleared. The dough stuck to the pan because we had not put
enough flour underneath. The temperature was too low in some places and
overcooked in others, because the rest of us could not shape the dough
by hand, we had to use the squeezing machine. None of it was right. None
of it was Sam.

When he came back, he was thinner. His hair was falling out. He laughed
loudly, his voice trembling slightly. When I told him I was leaving the
job, he said no worries, because he was leaving too. He was applying for
an operations assistant or management role in the on-campus residency
system. That position would give him 500 dollars more per month. I told
him, genuinely excited, that I was also entering a new chapter to become
a writing tutor, which would raise my wage by three dollars an hour. He
laughed again. His son is turning fifteen, he said. He wants to give him
a better education, a better life. After rent and child support, there
is nearly nothing left.

My zine was created for a Chinese-language literary reading event
organized by a Seattle-based reading community. It was designed for
English-speaking UW students, readers who might be curious about Chinese
culture and contemporary literature but have little prior exposure to
either. The book at the center of the event is *I Deliver Parcels in
Beijing* by Hu Anyan, a nonfiction collection about nineteen
unremarkable jobs, written by a man who spent twenty years doing labor
work before he began writing. The book topped China's most prestigious
book chart in 2023, sold over 200,000 copies, and was named a Best Book
of the Year by both the Financial Times and The Economist.

In this essay, I am expanding that original message to a wider audience,
to anyone who has ever asked why they work, what they are working
toward, and whether the life they are living is the one they actually
chose. What I want to argue is something I did not fully understand
until Sam came back from the hospital thinner, trembling, laughing.

I am thinking about what writing does for people whose lives are not
supposed to be written about. I am thinking about what nonfiction means
to ordinary people --- whether the small details of a life spent in
labor are trivial, or whether they contain something that more
celebrated lives do not. I have been trying to understand this through
my own experience: working in the school dining hall, meeting chefs and
full-time workers from many different backgrounds, using literature and
part-time work to approach the imagination of another way of living.

The question I keep returning to is this: how dare anyone call these
downward lives? The horizon determines the height --- and what I see,
when I look at Sam, when I read Hu Anyan, is a tenacious, upward
vitality. Within the space available to them, they are already reaching
for the farthest and most beautiful future they can foresee. They give
the people they love the best of what they have. In a world that fills
their days with mechanical labor, with survival space compressed to its
limits, they still hold to their own moral lines, still pursue their own
inner lives, still extend kindness to strangers they have just met.

Who could call this a lower life?

Hu Anyan spent twenty years doing labor work before he began writing. So
did Sam, more or less --- twenty years at the pizza station, starting at
Papa John's. What strikes me about both of them is not their similarity
in occupation but their similarity in attention. Both know their domain
completely. Hu knows what it means to deliver parcels in Beijing in the
rain, to wait in a hallway with packages while residents look through
the peephole and decide not to answer. Sam knows what it means to keep a
pizza station running alone, to read the oven's temperature by the color
of the crust, to throw dough into the air and catch it.

This is not trivial knowledge. It is not less serious than what happens
in university offices or research labs. It is just less visible --- less
written about, less counted, less rewarded.

Hu's book argues, through accumulation rather than declaration, that the
life of a laborer is a philosophical life. He reflects constantly on his
own experience --- what seems, from the outside, like cliché and
monotony. But through that reflection, he gradually discovers the
tension between himself and the world around him. He examines his own
boundaries and feelings. He tries to redefine what freedom means when
you have very little of it. Writing, for Hu, is what allows him to see
his own life clearly --- to step back from the repetition and ask what
it means.

Writing is generous in this way. It does not require a university degree
or a certain kind of life. It requires only attention --- the
willingness to look closely at your own experience and ask what it is
telling you. As soon as you begin to reflect on your own living, to
introspect with close attention, you can see the vitality in it. The
strength. The resilience. These are not qualities reserved for lives
lived at a certain altitude of social hierarchy. They are everywhere, in
every person trying to reach the farthest future they can see.

I think about the opposite shore of the city in *The Great Gatsby* ---
the valley of ashes, the grey land, the people who live there and are
never really seen. Fitzgerald gives us that world as backdrop, not
foreground. The novel is about Gatsby and Daisy and Tom, the people with
green lights at the end of their docks. The people in the valley are
texture, not subjects.

Hu Anyan's book refuses this. It insists that the people in the valley
are the subject. That their inner lives are as complex, as worthy of
literature, as anyone else's. That the question of how to live well ---
how to maintain dignity when survival space is compressed to its limit,
how to love a child you can only see for half a day each week, how to
keep making pizza when your hands are the only thing between the station
and chaos --- is not a lesser question. It is the question.

This is what I wanted the zine to say, and what I want this essay to say
more clearly: Hu Anyan's book is not a sociological document about
Chinese labor conditions. It is not a curiosity for people interested in
contemporary China. It is philosophy. It is as serious as anything
written from a position of comfort and stability --- perhaps more
serious, because it was written from a position where writing itself
required an act of will.

There should be no boundary between intellectual work and labor work ---
not in how we compensate people, and not in how we read them. Every
person's dignity and meaning of living should be genuinely recognized.
Sam told me this in his own way, on the day I left: remember, when you
get your business degree, pay the labor more. He said it laughing. But
he meant it. And I think he knew that I knew he meant it.

I feel the commonalities among people across continents. Hu and Sam have
never met. They do not speak the same language. Their cities --- Beijing
and Seattle --- look nothing alike. But both spent twenty years doing
labor work, and both know what it means to love someone they cannot
always reach. Both know that as soon as life gets on track, time moves
faster than you expected. Both are reaching.

Writing is a pause button. It is a way of stopping the motion long
enough to ask: what is this life I am living? Is this the one I chose?
What does it mean that I am here, in this place, doing this work,
knowing these people?

Hu Anyan asked these questions in a notebook, probably at night,
probably after a long shift. Sam has never written a book. But he told
me his life story across a pizza station, over the course of half a
year, in pieces --- North Carolina, the divorce, the custody, the
hospital, the son turning fifteen, the five hundred dollars. He
reflected. He shared. He witnessed his own life out loud, to anyone who
would listen carefully enough.

I tried to listen that carefully. I am still learning how.

What writing does for lives not supposed to be written about is this: it
makes them visible. Not as sociological data. Not as background texture.
As lives --- full, particular, reaching, worthy of the page.
