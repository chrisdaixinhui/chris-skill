**The Green-Headed Duck**

*encounter before being caught by categorization*

Gasworks Park was built on the ruins of a factory. You can still see it
if you know how to look at the old industrial towers rising from the
hill, rusted and enormous, surrounded now by grass and morning quiet. I
went for an early jog there, alone, before the city woke up. The wind
was mild. Cool against my face. The kind of morning that felt like it
belonged only to whoever was awake enough to find it.

I usually like being around people. I like to observe, overhear, and
notice the way someone moves or laughs. Proximity to others makes me
feel included in the world rather than passing through it. I even
recorded a podcast recently with friends, mostly about the satisfaction
of meeting people, meeting *detailed* people, not the abstraction of
them. But that morning at Gasworks, alone, I felt welcomed differently.
I streamed like a plant, I think it's the only way I can describe it,
flowing, without resistance, without the usual effort of being
perceived.

I stood at the water and looked across. On the other side of the lake,
perfectly visible, perfectly distant, was the rest of the city: the
Space Needle, the downtown towers, the glass headquarters of the tech
companies that have made Seattle what it is now. All of it held at the
other shore by the lake, like a boundary someone drew on purpose. The
civilized city, across the water. And on my side: a duck crossing the
road. A small bird is eating from a flower. A plane moving silently
above, grey against the blue.

I noticed, really noticed, that the duck had a green head. The kind of
green that seems almost unreasonable in that light. The bird had
rust-red feathers on its belly. The plane I registered only as *plane*
and let go. I noticed that I did this. I noticed the difference in my
attention around natural healing.

Moving with complete indifference to whether anyone was watching, only
these creatures, there were no humans around me. Not categorizing. Not
being categorized, completely indifferent to humans' value system. And I
felt, standing at the seam between the old factory and the lake, between
what was built and what had grown back over it, I am a part of something
bigger.

The city across the lake is very good at legibility. That is, in part,
what it was built to do. The technology companies whose towers I could
see that morning have constructed systems that excel at the fastest
read, the optimization of a person into the version most useful, most
legible, most returnable as data. Isn't this a facet of the alienation
of the human species\' power, depriving them of the uniqueness of the
individuals, while flattening them into products produced? We are
distilling people through our own mental filter, I keep thinking. We
extract their essence as quickly as possible, reduce them to skeletons
of who they are, and move on, believing we have understood something.
But this is merely the substitution of the label for encounter.

**Both in private life, shaped by digital platforms, and in public life,
shaped by strong rhetorical appeals, we are losing the capacity for
genuine encounter because we have learned to read people as categorical
symbols before meeting them, even worse, mistake that reading for
understanding.**

**Digital platforms deepen alienation in private life because they
optimize for the most algorithmically legible version of a person,
training us to pre-categorize before genuine encounter.** It is the best
age and the worst age. We have more access to the world than any
generation before us, more information, more perspectives, more faces,
and yet souls are more isolated, concentrations more fragmented, the
sense of genuine encounter rarer. The speed of it all makes us feel that
understanding is happening. The algorithm serves you the world, and the
world arrives. Pre-sorted, pre-confirmed, pre-known, you never have to
sit with the not-yet-known. Echo chambers consolidate the illusion of
engagement rather than truth-building. You never have to let the
green-headed duck surprise you, because the category *duck* was already
loaded before you looked. Imagine how many times it happened that you
had already foreseen the purpose and presumed goal of a future encounter
with people.

John Horton, writing in 1964, argued that the concept of alienation had
been quietly drained of its radical humanistic content, transformed from
a critique of systems that separate people from themselves and each
other into a neutral psychological variable, a personal feeling rather
than a structural condition. Something similar has happened to encounter
itself, along with the panic of living in an accelerating world. Social
tensions are rising exponentially alongside the pace of technology, as I
have been thinking about lately, that panic evolves into alienation. And
alienation evolves into a defense mechanism: we abstract the other
person before they can abstract us.

We distill people into their most algorithmically legible form. Their
most shareable, most triggering, most categorizable "essence". The full
person, with their dialect, their uniqueness, their cumbersome and
gratefulness, can never pass through the prebuilt mental pipeline. Only
the skeleton arrives. How can this be viewed as the "essence"? When I
encounter someone, I think the first question that comes to mind is:
what is their value to us, and *what do they represent as a symbol?* We
no longer treat humans as humans, but as a compounding product of
symbols. How could this be treated as sophistication? It is a wound that
presents itself as worldliness.

**Kirk\'s campus rhetoric exemplifies how public alienation operates. It
confirms audiences in what they already believe rather than opening them
to genuine encounter, and in doing so, it enacts a subtle but real
violence against everyone it names.** Charlie Kirk is perhaps the most
fluent contemporary practitioner of this operation on a political scale.
His campus rhetoric is precisely engineered to make his audiences feel
that they already understand the people being discussed, immigrants,
activists, academics, the left, the other side, before any encounter
with those people has taken place. These persuasions, though
successfully effective, are merely manipulations of symbols in the
logocentric language system.

Jonathan Leader Maynard and Aliza Luft, writing on dehumanization, argue
that dehumanization is neither the animalistic nor the blatant
categorization to the "inhuman" edge, nor an effect took place in
isolated individuals reacting to diffuse rhetoric. It could be based on
ideological and ecological contexts, operating by flattening people into
roles that the rhetoric system needs them to play. Such binary narration
cultivates the seed of polarization and hatred as it undermines humans'
completeness, denying their multifaceted nature.

What makes this paradoxical, as Maynard and Luft note, is that
dehumanization is often realized through highly moralized judgment (the
target is called criminal, traitor, enemy) terms that are meaningless
when applied to something truly inhuman, but meaningful and wounding
when applied to a person who is, in fact, fully human. Not claiming
these people as "subhuman", though, Kirk's audience is simply treated as
instruments of his persuasion effects. The hidden layer of this is the
mismatch of power. Naturally and implicitly, his persuasion becomes the
imposition of violence since the audience is placed at a lower level of
social hierarchy.

Kirk's method performs the same foreclosure in real time. He does not
invite his audiences to encounter a perspective different from their
own. He confirms what they already believe they know. He strips both his
audience and the signified group in his arguments of their irreducible
individuality and makes them legible as political concepts. Just as
Horton argued that contemporary discourse strips concepts of their
humanistic content, making alienation "value-free," which quietly
renders it conformist. And because his audiences are largely young
people, college students, people still building the frameworks through
which they will understand the world for decades, what gets confirmed
and thickened in them matters enormously. They leave his events not
opened, but more sealed, more furious. Lonelier, more silent, even if
louder, aggressively, perhaps the next generation of silenced patriots.

**What would a humanistic rhetoric look like?**

Someone might reasonably push back here. All rhetoric, they might say,
works through abstraction. To address an *audience* is already to
address a category, because an audience is a collective. Persuasion has
always moved people in groups, not one by one. Kirk is simply doing what
every effective communicator does. Through the strategic identification
of common ground, he built upon their shared experience and met people
where they are. To demand that rhetoric make space for individual
encounter is idealistic, impractical, maybe even naive.

There is a difference between a rhetoric that moves upward from the
particular toward something genuinely shared. This could be a claim or
an observation that begins with the specific and opens outward into
common humanity. And a rhetoric that moves downward from the category,
confirming people in what they already believe and calling that truth.
Ruth Bader Ginsburg was also a rhetorician working at enormous scale,
but her arguments were built on the irreducibly specific. In *Weinberger
v. Wiesenfeld* (1975), she represented a widower denied Social Security
benefits automatically granted to widows. Her strategy was simply to
make him real: a specific man, a grieving father holding his newborn
son. The Court ruled unanimously in his favor, because you cannot look
at a fully rendered person and still treat them as a category.
Presenting the whole picture of the truth with more evidence we
collected, the argument will follow naturally.

Admittedly, abstraction is not the enemy itself; however, it's not
always alienating as soon as one realizes the incompleteness embedded
within. My claim is that abstraction, which presents itself as
*complete,* is what alienates. To persuade someone should be to
negotiate with them, to develop a shared truth together, not to act upon
them as though they were receptors waiting to be filled. The message is
as important as its effect. A rhetoric that treats its audience as means
rather than as participants is not persuasion. It is a more polite name
for something else.

**Creatures, places, and people are irreducible because no category can
account for what is encountered when you reach them directly.**

I think about the city across the lake. The towers caught the early
light, and there was nothing hostile in the view. The systems built on
that shore are human systems of speed and scale. They require the plane
to be *vehicle*, the unknown to be *type*, and all the audience to be
*demographic* (waiting to be addressed). Abstraction is the machinery of
understanding; without it, we could not compare, could not reason, could
not build anything. There is no room for the green-headed duck crossing
the road at Gasworks at seven in the morning in such a system. He would
be simply treated as the noise in the algorithm.

But I am saying: don't say you know someone when you have only named
them. Don't treat a person as something static. Everything is always
changing, entangling, flowing. The duck with the green head is not the
same duck he was yesterday, even if the category is the same.

The duck crossed the road and disappeared into the grass. The
red-bellied bird finished eating and flew. The plane passed over and was
gone. The city across the lake gleamed and went about its business. And
I stood between all of it, between the rusted towers and the rippling
water, and felt that I was part of some entanglement that had not yet
been abstracted, just briefly. Something still whole.

I think this is what a genuine encounter feels like. I think this is
what we keep almost having and keep losing.

Chris Dai
